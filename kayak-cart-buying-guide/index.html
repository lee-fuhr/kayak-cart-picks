How to choose a kayak cart

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best kayak cart for 2026 depends on your kayak’s weight, hull shape, and where you launch. For most recreational kayaks, a scupper-pin or strap-on cart with large pneumatic wheels offers the best combination of stability and rollability. If you primarily launch from sandy beaches, consider a cart with wide balloon tires and a corrosion-resistant frame.

What type of kayak cart should I buy?

There are three main styles: scupper‑pin carts that insert into the drainage holes of sit‑on‑top kayaks, strap‑on saddle carts that cradle the hull, and hybrid carts with adjustable cradles. Scupper‑pin carts are the most secure for sit‑on‑top models because they lock into existing holes and keep the cart centered, but they require your kayak to have scupper holes that align with the cart’s pins. Saddle carts work for any kayak shape, sit‑on‑top, sit‑inside, inflatable, but they need a good strap system to avoid slipping. Hybrid carts offer adjustable cradles and are versatile for mixed kayak fleets. The key is matching the cart type to your kayak’s hull design and your tolerance for extra straps or pins.

How much weight capacity do I need?

The cart’s capacity must exceed your kayak’s total weight, including gear you might load while it is on the cart. Many recreational kayaks weigh 40 to 70 pounds, so a cart rated for 100 pounds is usually adequate. Tandem or fishing kayaks can weigh 80 to 120 pounds or more, so look for a cart with a rating of 150 pounds or higher. Do not just match the rating to your kayak’s dry weight; add 15–25 pounds for a paddle, cooler, or fishing crate you may strap on during transport. Overloading a cart warps the frame, flats the tires, and makes rolling on soft ground nearly impossible.

Wheel types: pneumatic vs solid vs balloon

Wheel choice is the biggest factor in how your cart performs on different surfaces. Pneumatic (air‑filled) tires are the most versatile, they cushion bumps, roll easily on pavement, and provide moderate flotation on sand if they are wide enough. Solid rubber tires never go flat but ride rough and dig into soft sand. Balloon tires (very wide, low‑pressure) are the best for sand and mud because they distribute weight and float on top; they are bulkier and heavier, though, and can be overkill if you only launch from concrete ramps. For most mixed use, choose a cart with wide pneumatic tires, around 8 to 10 inches in diameter, and keep a small pump for adjustments.

Frame materials and corrosion resistance

Since kayak carts are used near salt or fresh water, frame material matters for longevity. Steel frames are heavy but cheap and strong; they will rust unless you rinse them after every saltwater trip and occasionally apply rust inhibitor. Aluminum frames are lighter and naturally corrosion‑resistant, ideal for saltwater paddlers, but can dent under extreme loads. Stainless steel offers the best strength‑to‑corrosion balance, though it costs more and adds a bit of weight. Plastic or polymer frames are completely rust‑proof and lightweight, but they flex under heavy tandem kayaks. If you launch in saltwater more than a few times a year, skip steel and go with aluminum or stainless steel.

How to choose between scupper-pin and strap-on carts

If you own a sit‑on‑top kayak with scupper holes that are evenly spaced and accessible, a scupper‑pin cart gives you the most stable, hands‑free attachment, no straps to tighten, no risk of the cart sliding sideways. The downside is that not all kayak scupper holes line up with cart pins, and the pins can wear the scupper hole edges over time. Strap‑on saddle carts work with any hull shape and are easier to fit on multiple kayaks, but they require you to cinch straps firmly and check them during rolling; a loose strap lets the cart drift sideways. For a fleet with mixed hull types, a strap‑on cart with padded cradles is more flexible. For a single sit‑on‑top kayak, measure the scupper hole spacing first, then decide.

Storage and portability considerations

A kayak cart you only use at the launch still has to get from your car to the water. Collapsible models that fold flat or have removable wheels fit in a trunk or hatch easily, but they often have smaller wheels and less robust frames. Carts that stay assembled are quicker to deploy but take up more space. If you plan to leave the cart at home when you paddle, foldability is a priority. If you want the cart strapped to your kayak for use at multiple put‑ins, a permanently assembled cart with quick‑release axles is better. Also check the assembled weight: a cart over 15 pounds becomes another heavy thing to carry, defeating its purpose.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a kayak cart on soft sand?

Yes, but only if the cart has wide balloon tires or very wide pneumatic tires at low pressure. Narrow wheels will sink, even with a light kayak. For deep, dry sand, look for tires at least 10 inches wide and reduce tire pressure to around 10 psi to improve flotation.

Will a cart damage my kayak’s hull?

A well‑designed cart with padded cradles, foam saddles, or rubber straps will not damage a polyethylene hull. Hard plastic or bare metal contact points can cause scratches or dents, especially on thin kayaks. Always inspect the cart’s contact surfaces and add pipe insulation foam if necessary.

Do I need a different cart for a sit‑on‑top vs sit‑inside kayak?

Not necessarily. Strap‑on saddle carts work for both types. Scupper‑pin carts only fit sit‑on‑top kayaks that have scupper holes. Some sit‑inside kayaks have a recessed keel area where a strap‑on cart sits securely; just make sure the cart’s cradle does not contact the hull’s thin side walls.

How do I secure the kayak to the cart?

For scupper‑pin carts, simply insert the pins into the scupper holes; no additional fastening is needed. For strap‑on or saddle carts, thread the included straps around the hull and through the cart’s frame, then tighten them until the kayak cannot shift side to side or bounce up. Some carts have cam‑buckle straps for quick tension adjustment.

Can I leave the cart on the kayak while on the water?

Most kayak carts are not designed to stay attached during paddling. The wheels create drag, the frame can unbalance the kayak, and straps could loosen. If you need to launch from a difficult spot and want the cart ready for the return, use a quick‑release cart that you can stow onboard, or leave the cart on shore and retrieve it later.

How do I measure my kayak for a cart?

If you want a scupper‑pin cart, measure the center‑to‑center distance between the two scupper holes closest to where you want the cart to sit (usually near the cockpit or just behind it). For a strap‑on cart, measure the width of your kayak’s hull at the same location to ensure the cradles or arms can accommodate it. Also check the height of the keel or any skegs that might interfere with the cart’s axle.

In shortA well‑chosen kayak cart transforms a back‑aching carry into a smooth roll to the water. Prioritize wheel type and frame durability based on your typical terrain, and always measure your kayak’s scupper holes or hull shape to ensure a snug fit. A few minutes of research now will save you years of lugging.
